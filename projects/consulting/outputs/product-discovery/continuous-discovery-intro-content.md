## Slide 03 — The Problem with Traditional Product Development

**Headline:** Discovery done once is discovery done wrong

**Content — two columns:**

*The old way:*
- Research phase at start of project, then stop
- Product managers write specs, hand off to designers and engineers
- Big bets on features built over months
- Customer feedback arrives too late to change course
- Teams optimise for output: shipping features

*The result:*
- Features nobody uses
- Long feedback loops
- Teams building solutions to the wrong problems
- Discovery treated as a one-time gate, not a continuous habit

**Speaker notes:**
Most teams have done this. The intent is good — research before you build. The problem is the cadence. When discovery happens once, at the start, assumptions calcify. By the time a feature ships, the team has forgotten why they made half the decisions they made. The world has moved on. Customers have moved on.

## Slide 04 — What is Continuous Discovery?

**Headline:** Discovery is not a phase. It's a habit.

**Definition:**
Continuous discovery is the practice of conducting small research activities on a regular cadence — at minimum weekly — by the team who builds the product.

**Three defining characteristics:**

1. **Continuous** — not a phase, a recurring habit embedded in how the team works
2. **Team-led** — not delegated to a researcher; the trio who builds the product does the discovering
3. **Outcome-focused** — research is always connected to a specific business outcome the team is trying to drive

**Key principle:**
> You can't be customer-centric without being in continuous contact with customers.

**Speaker notes:**
The word "continuous" does a lot of work here. It doesn't mean talking to customers every hour. It means the habit of customer contact never fully stops. At minimum, the team is having at least one customer conversation per week. This cadence is what makes insights fresh, decisions fast, and assumptions visible.

## Slide 05 — The Shift: From Outputs to Outcomes

**Headline:** Stop measuring what you ship. Start measuring what changes.

**Content — three contrast pairs:**

| Output mindset | Outcome mindset |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "We shipped 12 features this quarter" | "Checkout conversion increased by 8%" |
| "We delivered the roadmap" | "Customers are completing the task faster" |
| "We finished on time" | "We reduced support tickets by 30%" |

**Definition:**
An **outcome** is a change in customer behaviour that drives business results. It is not a feature, a project, or a deliverable.

**Why it matters:**
When a team is assigned an outcome instead of a feature, they are free to discover the best solution — rather than executing a predetermined one.

**Speaker notes:**
This is one of the most significant mindset shifts the framework asks of teams and organisations alike. Roadmaps built around features lock teams into solutions before they understand problems. Roadmaps built around outcomes give teams the mandate to discover.

## Slide 06 — Connecting Discovery to Strategy: The Progress Wheel *(Tim Herbig)*

**Headline:** Discovery without strategy is just exploring. Strategy without discovery is just guessing.

**Framework: Tim Herbig's Progress Wheel**
Tim Herbig's Progress Wheel (from *Real Progress*, 2024) connects three domains into a single reinforcing loop:

```
        STRATEGY
       ↙         ↖
  DISCOVERY  →  OKRs
```

**How the three elements connect:**

- **Strategy → OKRs:** Strategy prevents OKRs from becoming generic metrics. It defines the competitive position — *why* the team is targeting this market — which gives OKRs meaningful context.
- **OKRs → Discovery:** Clearly defined OKRs focus discovery toward the most critical uncertainties. Without OKRs, discovery sprawls. With them, every interview and test has a purpose.
- **Discovery → Strategy:** When strategy feels unclear or stale, validated problems from discovery inform and refine strategic direction. Discovery doesn't just serve strategy — it feeds it.

**The connection to Torres' OST:**
The *outcome* at the top of the Opportunity Solution Tree is where Torres and Herbig connect. The outcome is the OKR-level signal that defines which customer opportunities are worth pursuing. Strategy decides the playing field; OKRs define the metric; discovery navigates the terrain.

> "Just because a user problem exists does not mean that you need to solve it."
> — Tim Herbig

**Speaker notes:**
Teams often treat strategy, OKRs, and discovery as three separate rituals done by different people at different times. Herbig's key insight is that they are one system. The Progress Wheel diagnoses where teams are stuck: if discovery is unfocused, check the OKRs; if OKRs feel arbitrary, check the strategy; if strategy feels theoretical, go back to discovery. The wheel turns in both directions.

## Slide 07 — The Product Trio

**Headline:** Discovery is a team sport, not a PM's job

**Content:**
The product trio is the core unit of continuous discovery. It consists of:

- **Product Manager** — defines and owns the outcome; connects business strategy to customer needs
- **Design Lead** — visualises opportunities and solutions; leads interview sessions
- **Tech Lead** — assesses feasibility; brings technical creativity to solution ideation

**How they work together:**
- All three participate in customer interviews — not just the PM or researcher
- All three contribute to the Opportunity Solution Tree
- All three vote on which opportunities and solutions to pursue

**Why it matters:**
When only one person does discovery, knowledge is siloed. When the whole trio discovers together, the team makes faster, better-aligned decisions — and everyone is invested in the outcome.

**Speaker notes:**
Teresa Torres is clear on this: the product trio should do discovery together, not sequentially. The designer doesn't wait for the PM to finish research. The engineer isn't handed a spec. They explore together from the start. This is what makes the team move fast without moving wrong.

## Slide 08 — What is an Opportunity?

**Headline:** Opportunities live in your customers — not in your backlog

**Definition:**
An opportunity is any customer need, pain point, or desire that, if addressed, could help the team achieve its desired outcome.

**Three types:**

1. **Needs** — what customers are trying to accomplish; jobs they want done
2. **Pain points** — friction, frustration, or barriers they experience today
3. **Desires** — things they wish existed; gaps they feel but haven't articulated

**Key distinction:**
> Opportunities are not solutions. They are the *reason* a solution would be valuable.

**Common mistake:**
Jumping straight to solutions before the opportunity is clearly understood. Teams that skip this step build the right thing for the wrong reason — or the wrong thing altogether.

**Speaker notes:**
The word "opportunity" is intentional. It reframes customer problems as chances to create value. When you think in opportunities, you stay curious. When you think in features, you start defending decisions you haven't made yet.

## Slide 09 — The Opportunity Space

**Headline:** Mapping the full landscape of customer needs

**Content:**
The opportunity space is the full set of customer needs, pain points, and desires uncovered through continuous research. It is not a list — it is a structured hierarchy.

**Key properties:**

- **Nested** — broad opportunities contain sub-opportunities; sub-opportunities can be broken down further
- **Discovered, not invented** — opportunities come from customers, not from internal brainstorming
- **Evolving** — new interviews surface new opportunities; the space changes over time
- **Strategy-filtered** — not every opportunity belongs in the tree; only those connected to the current outcome and strategic direction

**Example hierarchy:**
```
"Customers struggle to manage their finances" (broad)
  └── "Hard to track spending across multiple accounts" (mid)
        └── "No single view of subscriptions" (specific)
```

**Speaker notes:**
The opportunity space prevents teams from being overwhelmed. You don't have to solve everything — you have to choose well. The hierarchy helps the team understand scope and prioritise without losing sight of the bigger picture. The strategy filter (from Slide 06) is what keeps the tree from sprawling indefinitely.

## Slide 10 — The Opportunity Solution Tree (Overview)

**Headline:** One diagram to connect outcome, opportunities, solutions, and tests

**Description:**
The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) is the central tool of continuous discovery. It is a living visual map that connects:

```
Desired Outcome
    └── Opportunity Space
            └── Solutions
                    └── Assumptions & Experiments
```

**What it does:**
- Keeps the team focused on a single outcome
- Makes the opportunity space visible and navigable
- Shows which solutions are connected to which opportunities
- Surfaces the assumptions that need testing before anything is built

**Why it is called a "living" document:**
The OST is never finished. As interviews surface new needs, as tests reveal new information, the tree grows and shifts. It is a map of what the team currently knows — and doesn't know.

**Speaker notes:**
The OST replaces the traditional feature roadmap as the team's primary planning artefact during discovery. The roadmap shows *what will be built*. The OST shows *what is being learned*. Both have a role — but discovery lives in the tree. The outcome at the top is where Tim Herbig's OKRs land — it is the strategic signal that focuses everything below it.

## Slide 11 — OST Layer 1: The Outcome

**Headline:** Start with the outcome — everything else follows

**Definition:**
The outcome is the business need the team has been asked to address. It defines the scope of discovery and determines which customer opportunities are relevant.

**A good outcome:**
- Is measurable — it changes in a way the team can detect
- Is connected to customer behaviour — not internal activity
- Is owned by the team — not handed down as a feature request
- Is derived from strategy — it reflects the playing field the business has chosen

**Examples:**

| Weak (output-framed) | Strong (outcome-framed) |
|----------------------|-------------------------|
| Launch onboarding flow v2 | Increase 30-day retention from 40% to 55% |
| Build reporting dashboard | Increase weekly active usage by power users |
| Redesign checkout | Reduce cart abandonment by 15% |

**Speaker notes:**
The outcome is the lens through which everything else is evaluated. A customer need only becomes an opportunity if addressing it could plausibly drive the outcome. This is what keeps discovery focused rather than exploratory for its own sake. Note: the outcome should trace back to company strategy — if the team can't explain why this outcome matters strategically, that's a signal the strategy work needs to happen first.

## Slide 12 — OST Layer 2: Opportunities

**Headline:** Opportunities are discovered — not invented

**Where opportunities come from:**
Opportunities emerge from customer interviews. Each interview surfaces stories — real experiences the customer has had — and within those stories are the needs, pain points, and desires that form the opportunity space.

**Building the opportunity space:**
- Run weekly interviews (detailed in Slides 15–17)
- After each interview, capture the opportunities surfaced
- Add them to the OST under the relevant parent opportunity
- Over time, the map fills in and patterns emerge

**How to evaluate an opportunity:**
1. Is it real? (Based on actual customer behaviour, not stated preference)
2. Is it big enough? (How many customers have this need? How often?)
3. Is it connected to the outcome? (Would solving this drive the metric?)
4. Is it on the playing field? (Does it align with the team's strategic direction?)

**Speaker notes:**
One of the most common mistakes teams make is populating the opportunity space from internal brainstorming rather than customer interviews. Internal ideas can surface hypotheses — but opportunities must be validated by customer evidence before they earn a place in the tree. The fourth evaluation criterion — strategic alignment — comes from Herbig: not every real customer problem belongs in this team's OST.

## Slide 13 — OST Layer 3: Solutions

**Headline:** Generate many solutions. Commit to none — yet.

**The rule:**
For each opportunity you target, generate at least three distinct solutions before evaluating any of them. Diversity of solutions matters more than quantity.

**Why three?**
- One solution is a bet
- Two solutions is a dilemma
- Three solutions opens up creative comparison and prevents premature commitment

**How to generate good solutions:**
- Time-box ideation (15–20 minutes per opportunity)
- Include the full trio — engineers see technical possibilities designers miss; designers see UX options PMs miss
- Think across modalities: UI change, notification, onboarding, pricing, workflow, integration

**What solutions are not:**
Solutions are not project plans or specs. At this stage, they are hypotheses. They become real only when assumptions have been tested.

**Speaker notes:**
The discipline of generating multiple solutions is one of the hardest habits for teams to build. There's a strong pull toward the first plausible idea. The OST makes this visible: if you have only one solution under an opportunity, you haven't really ideated yet.

## Slide 14 — OST Layer 4: Assumptions & Experiments

**Headline:** What must be true for this solution to work?

**Definition:**
An assumption is any belief the team holds that, if false, would cause the solution to fail.

**Four types of assumptions:**
1. **Desirability** — Do customers want this?
2. **Viability** — Does it work for the business?
3. **Feasibility** — Can the team build it?
4. **Usability** — Can customers use it effectively?

**The assumption testing process:**
1. List all assumptions behind each of your top solutions
2. Identify the riskiest assumption (the one that, if false, kills the solution)
3. Design the smallest possible test to evaluate it
4. Run the test — collect evidence — update the OST

**Key principle:**
> Test your riskiest assumption first. If it fails, you've saved the cost of building the wrong thing.

**Speaker notes:**
Assumption testing is not user research. It is not a full prototype test or a focus group. It can be a landing page, a fake door, a five-minute concept sketch shown to three customers, or a quick data query. The goal is evidence, fast.

## Slide 15 — Continuous Interviewing: The Weekly Habit

**Headline:** Talk to at least one customer every week. No exceptions.

**The cadence:**
- At minimum: one customer interview per week, per product trio
- Ideally: 2–3 per week as the habit matures
- Each interview: 30–60 minutes, focused on a specific topic or opportunity area

**Who recruits?**
This is one of the most common blockers. Teresa Torres recommends building an ongoing opt-in pool of customers willing to talk regularly — a "continuous interview pipeline" — rather than recruiting from scratch each week.

**Who attends?**
All three members of the product trio. Not observers behind glass. Not a researcher relaying findings. The trio in the room (or on the call) together.

**Why weekly?**
- Insights stay fresh
- Patterns emerge faster
- The team builds intuition about customers over time
- Assumptions are challenged before they become baked-in decisions

**Speaker notes:**
The biggest objection Teresa hears is "we don't have time." The answer is that weekly interviewing does not require a full research programme. A 30-minute conversation once a week is enough to keep the team connected. The trap is treating it as optional — it has to become as non-negotiable as the weekly team standup.

## Slide 16 — How to Interview: Story-Based Questions

**Headline:** Ask about the past. Never ask about the future.

**The core technique: Story-based interviewing**
Instead of asking what customers want or what they would do, ask them to share a specific recent experience. This surfaces real behaviour, not hypothetical preference.

**The golden question:**
> "Tell me about the last time you [experienced this situation]."

**Follow-up probes:**
- "What happened right before that?"
- "What did you do next?"
- "How did you feel at that point?"
- "What were you hoping would happen?"

**Avoid these questions:**
- "Would you use a feature that…?" (hypothetical)
- "How often do you…?" (generalisation, not specific)
- "What do you want?" (solution-seeking, not need-seeking)

**Why stories?**
Stories reveal behaviour as it actually happened. When customers describe what they *did*, they can't retrofit the answer they think you want to hear. Specific stories are more reliable than general statements.

**Speaker notes:**
This is Teresa Torres' most practical and immediately applicable technique. You can run a better interview today just by changing one thing: start with "tell me about the last time…" instead of "what do you think about…"

## Slide 17 — The Interview Snapshot

**Headline:** Synthesise immediately. Don't let insights get lost.

**What is an interview snapshot?**
A single-page document completed immediately after each interview. It captures the story that emerged and the opportunities it surfaced.

**Snapshot structure:**
1. **Customer context** — who they are, their role, their situation
2. **The story** — a brief narrative of what happened in the interview (their experience, in sequence)
3. **Opportunities surfaced** — needs, pain points, or desires that emerged from the story
4. **Key quotes** — direct quotes that carry the most signal
5. **OST additions** — which opportunities to add or update in the tree

**The habit:**
Complete the snapshot within one hour of the interview. The longer the team waits, the more detail is lost. Many teams block 15 minutes immediately after the call.

**Speaker notes:**
Most interview insights never make it into any decision. The snapshot is the fix. It creates a searchable, shareable record of what was learned. Over time, a library of snapshots becomes one of the most valuable assets a product team has — a real map of customer experience accumulated over months and years.

## Slide 18 — Assumption Testing: Test Small, Learn Fast

**Headline:** Don't build. Test.

**The mindset:**
Every solution the team considers is built on assumptions. Most will never be tested — they'll just be built. Continuous discovery reverses this: test assumptions before committing to build.

**How to design a test:**

| Step | Question |
|------|----------|
| 1. List assumptions | What must be true for this solution to work? |
| 2. Rank by risk | Which assumption, if false, kills this solution? |
| 3. Design the test | What is the smallest way to evaluate it? |
| 4. Set a threshold | What evidence would change our mind? |
| 5. Run and evaluate | Did we get that evidence? |

**Types of tests (examples):**
- **Fake door / smoke test** — does anyone click on something that doesn't exist yet?
- **Concept test** — show a sketch to 5 customers; does it make sense?
- **Data analysis** — does the behaviour pattern we assumed actually exist in the data?
- **Wizard of Oz** — manually simulate the experience before automating it

**Speaker notes:**
The instinct to build first and test later is deeply rooted in most teams. The counter-argument is simple: if you can test a riskiest assumption in two days for zero cost, why would you spend six weeks building something that might be built on a false premise?

## Slide 19 — Prioritising Opportunities

**Headline:** You can't solve everything. Choose well.

**How to prioritise opportunities in the OST:**

Teresa Torres recommends evaluating each opportunity on three dimensions:

1. **Opportunity size** — How many customers are affected? How often?
2. **Business value** — How strongly is this opportunity connected to the team's outcome?
3. **Satisfaction gap** — How poorly is this need currently being met? (High gap = high opportunity)

**The key move:**
Rather than ranking all opportunities in a list, the team identifies a **target opportunity** — one opportunity the team will focus on for the current cycle. This creates clarity and prevents spreading effort too thin.

**When to change the target opportunity:**
- When tests suggest the opportunity is smaller than expected
- When interviews surface a higher-signal opportunity
- When the business context shifts and a different outcome is prioritised

**Speaker notes:**
One of the most common failure modes in continuous discovery is trying to work on too many opportunities at once. The target opportunity disciplines the team. You can maintain awareness of the full opportunity space while moving with focus.

## Slide 20 — The Full Continuous Discovery Loop

**Headline:** The cycle that never stops

**The loop:**

```
Set Outcome (from Strategy + OKRs)
    ↓
Conduct Weekly Interviews
    ↓
Map Opportunities into OST
    ↓
Select Target Opportunity
    ↓
Generate Solutions (minimum 3)
    ↓
Map & Test Assumptions
    ↓
Validate or Discard Solution
    ↓
Build (if validated)
    ↓
Measure Impact on Outcome
    ↓
(back to: Conduct Weekly Interviews)
```

**Key properties of the loop:**
- It runs in parallel with delivery — not before or after
- The outcome may evolve as strategy evolves; the loop adapts
- Each cycle makes the team sharper: better interviews, better OSTs, faster tests

**Speaker notes:**
This is the full picture. Continuous discovery is not a method you follow once and declare done. It is a system of habits that compounds over time. Teams that have been running this loop for 12 months make dramatically better product decisions than teams in month one — because they know their customers, they've tested hundreds of assumptions, and they've built intuition you can't fake. Notice also where the loop connects back to Herbig's wheel: measuring impact on the outcome feeds back into refining the strategy.

## Slide 21 — Continuous Discovery in Cybersecurity *(Domain Perspective)*

**Headline:** Security products have users too — discover what they actually need

**Why cybersecurity teams need continuous discovery:**
Security products are notoriously built for threat models rather than for the humans who operate them. The result: powerful tools with poor usability, adoption barriers, and security fatigue. Continuous discovery addresses this directly.

**Who are the "customers" in security?**
The product trio must be clear on who they are discovering for:

| Customer type | Examples | Key discovery focus |
|--------------|----------|---------------------|
| Internal operators | SOC analysts, incident responders, security engineers | Alert fatigue, investigation workflow friction, tooling gaps |
| External buyers | CISOs, IT managers, compliance officers | Risk visibility, audit trail completeness, reporting pain |
| End users | Employees using security-facing products | Friction in authentication, unclear policies, compliance burden |

**Where cybersecurity parallels the OST:**

- **Threat modeling ≈ Assumption testing** — both ask "what must be true for this to be safe/effective?" and identify where the risk lives
- **Vulnerability discovery ≈ Opportunity discovery** — both are systematic, evidence-based searches for gaps in the current state
- **Security requirements as constraints** — certain opportunities (e.g., improving speed at the cost of audit logging) may be strategically off-limits; the strategy filter applies here directly

**Special considerations for security discovery:**
1. **Consent and data sensitivity** — Interviews about security workflows may surface sensitive operational details. Obtain explicit consent; anonymise snapshots; do not record without permission.
2. **Bias toward compliance, not usability** — Security teams often interview compliance leads, not end users. The people writing the requirements are not the people experiencing the friction. Interview both.
3. **The "shadow IT" signal** — When users work around security tooling (using personal devices, unapproved apps), that is the highest-signal opportunity in the entire opportunity space. Treat it as such — not as a compliance failure to suppress, but as a discovery finding to investigate.
4. **Assume adversarial users exist** — When generating solutions, add a fifth assumption type alongside Desirability, Viability, Feasibility, Usability: **Security** — could a malicious actor exploit this solution pathway?

**Example opportunity space (security product team):**
```
Outcome: Reduce mean time to detect (MTTD) threats by 40%
  └── Analysts miss low-severity alerts due to volume (pain)
        └── No contextual triage scoring at alert level (specific)
  └── Investigation context requires switching between 4 tools (friction)
        └── No unified timeline view per entity (specific)
```

**Speaker notes:**
Continuous discovery is especially powerful in security because the cost of building the wrong thing is high — both in usability terms (tools that get ignored) and in security terms (tools that create false confidence). The OST forces security product teams to be explicit about what they're trying to change in human behaviour, not just what threats they're mitigating.

## Slide 22 — Getting Started + Key Takeaways

**Headline:** Start small. Start now. Don't wait for perfect.

**Five things the team can do this week:**

1. **Anchor to strategy** — Connect the team's current outcome to the company's strategic narrative. If the link is unclear, that is the first conversation to have.
2. **Schedule one interview** — Don't design a research programme. Just book one 30-minute call with a customer.
3. **Draw an OST** — Even with two or three opportunities so far, making the tree visible changes how the team thinks.
4. **Name three assumptions** — Pick the solution you're most confident about. List three things that must be true for it to work.
5. **Hold the weekly rhythm** — Block 30 minutes per week as "discovery time." Protect it like a standup.

**Key takeaways:**
- Discovery is a habit, not a phase
- Strategy sets the playing field; OKRs set the outcome; discovery navigates
- The product trio discovers together
- The OST connects everything — opportunity, solution, assumption, test
- Weekly interviews are the heartbeat of the system
